## Overview

Phisuea Samut (Thai: ผีเสื้อสมุทร, Nang Phisuea Samut) is a major character in the Thai epic poem Phra Aphai Mani by Sunthorn Phu (1786-1855), one of Thailand's most important literary works. She is a sea ogress (yak / phi) who lives in a cave beneath the sea. Drawn to the prince Aphai Mani by his magical flute (pi), she lulls his companions to sleep and abducts him to her undersea realm. Disguised in human form as a beautiful woman, she takes him as her husband and bears him a son, Sin Samut, whom she keeps isolated for eight years. The hero eventually deceives her into a long meditative fast on a distant island and escapes by sea; when she realises she has been deceived she reverts to her true ogress form and pursues him. Aphai Mani plays his magical flute, and the music breaks Phisuea Samut's heart, killing her. Phisuea Samut is treated in Thai literary tradition not only as a monster but as a tragic romantic figure. She is a tutelary figure of Ko Samet in Rayong Province, where statues commemorate her, and she appears in Thai theatre, illustration, and popular culture.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Thai. ผีเสื้อ (phisuea) modern Thai for 'butterfly' but here understood by literary commentators as derived from an older sense referencing an ancestral or guardian spirit; สมุทร (samut) meaning 'ocean / sea'.
- **Meaning**: Conventionally rendered as 'Sea Butterfly'; in context, 'Sea Spirit / Sea Ogress'.
- **First use**: First appears in Sunthorn Phu's Phra Aphai Mani, composed in the early-to-mid 19th century during the early Rattanakosin era.
